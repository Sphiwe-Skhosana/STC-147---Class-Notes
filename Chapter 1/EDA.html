What is Exploratory Data Analysis? (Lecture 1)

Exploratory data analysis (EDA) is the first and most important step in any data analysis. The goal of EDA is to understand the data by summarising its main characteristics. This must be done before any inference can be performed.

The goals of EDA include:

  • Understanding the structure of the data. This involves determining how many variables there are in the data, what their data type is (e.g.numerical, categorical) and what possible values they have (e.g.only positive values for a height variable; the number of categories in a categorical variable).

  • Assessing data quality. This includes determining if there are missing values, if there is enough data, and whether the data values appear correct. For instance, in a database of current employees of a business, a birth date such as 05/02/1897 is likely incorrect.

  • Data cleaning and wrangling. Here, obvious mistakes can be corrected, the shape of the data can be altered, or new columns can be created as needed. For example, if we had a dataset on countries, their area, and their population size, we could calculate a population proportion variable.

  • Detecting patterns in the data. In this step, we look for trends in the data and relationships between variables.

  • Detecting anomalies. This involves looking for unusual values and identifying outliers.

  • Checking assumptions. Before we begin analysing data, we might have certain expectations about the data. It is important to check whether these assumptions are valid.

  • Developing hypotheses. Exploring the data, looking for patterns and relationships, can help us think about which investigations are feasible, and what kind of hypotheses we might be able to test.

  • Selecting appropriate modelling techniques. Based on the structure of the data, outliers, assumptions, and other insights gleaned during EDA, we could identify potential ways of modelling the data for statistical inference.

It is important to note that EDA is all about describing data. During EDA, we are not looking for evidence to support hypotheses, or trying to force any kind of information out of the data. We are simply exploring the data as if it were a landscape.
